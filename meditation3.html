Thoughts on London's "Sea-Wolf"

Is the will to power admirable or reprehensible?

"I held that life was a ferment, a yeasty something which devoured life that it might live, and that living was merely successful piggishness.‚Äù

  I chose this book because I remember one scene vividly. It was when van Weyden, still naive, first witnessed Captain Wolf Larsen's contemptible treatment of his crew. Humphrey van Weyden, shocked, asked the captain if he had no respect for human life. The captain responded with "Life? Bah! Of cheap things it is the cheapest!" Those words were etched into my mind from the moment I read them. I was just as shocked as van Weyden to find such irreverent treatment justified by cruel materialist philosophy. As the years passed, I kept returning to those words, particularly when I discovered Nietzsche's idea of overcoming archaic traditions through the "Superman". Upon realizing that Larsen was an embodiment of the will to power, I felt compelled to revisit Jack London's novel, "The Sea Wolf". It has been interesting to witness this dichotomy between van Weyden's idealism and Larsen's grim realism. Ultimately, it has been edifying to witness a resolution to this dichotomy, despite being unsure of how it fits into Nietzschean philosophy, or if it does at all. Humphrey van Weyden does not descend to the debauchery of the sailors or the cruelty of Larsen because of the motivation to save a woman from Captain Larsen. It appears redemption of another leads to redemption from nihilism, or something worse. Ultimately, "The Sea Wolf" is a book that features the prominent Western motif of redemption in sacrifice, an archetype that goes all the way back to the Son of God himself.
